# Priority 1 Blocker Resolution Report - July 2026

This report synthesizes research findings regarding the Priority 1 blockers identified for the Signature Sovereign Solutions (SSS) economic model: Somaliland mineral royalties, India/Bihar import duties on bullion, and BIBC technical CAPEX/OPEX specifications. The objective is to provide actionable data to advance the economic model from benchmark data to a review-ready state.

## 1. Somaliland Mineral Royalties

Research into the Somaliland Mining and Minerals Policy 2025 [1] and the Ministry of Energy and Minerals website [2] indicates that specific, fixed royalty rates for strategic minerals are not explicitly detailed within the publicly available policy documents. Instead, the policy emphasizes a flexible approach:

> "Furthermore, the royalty regulations under the Mining Act provide for stability clauses under the mineral agreements as well as suspension, reduction and deferral of royalty payments in special circumstances which are additional incentives." [1]

And also states:

> "The minister responsible for mining and minerals shall from time to time stipulate the amount of royalty payable for a particular mineral and their respective pay-points such as Ex-mine gate or F.O.B. Different rates apply to different minerals. The minister responsible for mining and minerals shall publish royalty rates in legal notices or official gazette notices which are publicly available." [1]

While an older reference from the Ministry of Energy and Minerals website (dated August 14, 2020) mentioned royalties of 
3-5% of the mineral produced at Mine Gate or FOB [2], the 2025 policy indicates that specific rates are to be stipulated by the minister and published in legal notices. For the purpose of the economic model, a provisional royalty rate of **4%** (mid-range of the older guidance) will be used, with a note that this is subject to negotiation and official publication of specific rates for strategic minerals.

Investment incentives mentioned in the 2025 policy include:

*   Corporate tax at a 10% flat rate
*   Payroll tax at a 6% flat rate
*   Sales tax at 5%
*   3-year exemption from corporate tax
*   Tax exemption from imports of investment machinery and equipment
*   Special royalty considerations (reduction, suspension, and deferred payments)
*   Preferential agreements (mineral agreements with stability clauses, profit/benefit sharing agreements) [1]

## 2. India Bullion Import Duties

India's gold import duties have seen significant changes in 2026. As of May 22, 2026, the import duty on gold was sharply raised from 6% to 15%, marking the steepest increase on record [4]. This reverses a previous cut from 15% to 6% in July 2024 [3].

| Date        | Import Duty on Gold |
|-------------|---------------------|
| July 2024   | 6%                  |
| May 2026    | 15%                 |

This increase is part of a broader effort to moderate gold imports and conserve foreign exchange reserves [4]. While the basic customs duty (BCD) for gold is 15%, it's important to note that the overall duty can include an additional 1% Agriculture Infrastructure and Development Cess (AIDC) [3]. For the economic model, the current effective import duty on gold bullion will be considered **15%**.

Regarding Bihar-specific incentives for bullion, no direct incentives were found in the research. The Bihar Industrial & Investment Promotion Policy (BIIPP) 2025 offers general incentives for industrial investment, such as SGST reimbursement, capital subsidies, and land incentives [5, 6, 7]. However, these are not specifically tailored to bullion or gold refining operations.

## 3. BIBC Technical CAPEX/OPEX Specifications

Detailed technical CAPEX/OPEX specifications for the Bihar Integrated Bullion Complex (BIBC) were not found in the provided repository files or public search results. The `05_bibc_profile.md` file describes BIBC as a proposed asset/SPV with roles including LBMA-accredited refining, IFSCA vaulting, and BIS hallmarking, but lacks specific financial or technical details [8].

General gold refinery CAPEX benchmarks vary significantly based on capacity, technology, and location. For the purpose of the economic model, placeholder assumptions will be used for BIBC CAPEX/OPEX, pending further detailed engineering and financial feasibility studies. It is recommended to use industry benchmarks for similar-sized gold refineries in emerging markets as a starting point, and to clearly mark these as provisional.
