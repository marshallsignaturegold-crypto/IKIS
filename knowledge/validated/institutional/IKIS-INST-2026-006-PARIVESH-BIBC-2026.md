## 2. EIA REPORT STRUCTURE

### 2.0 Executive Summary

The Bihar Integrated Bullion Complex (BIBC) is a proposed Tier 3 Centralized Critical Minerals, Trade, Treasury, Reserve, Clearing, and Integrated Industrial Refining Complex located in the Patna–Gaya industrial corridor of Bihar, India. The project encompasses:

- **Gold Refinery:** 330 TPA capacity (1,000 kg/day) using fire assay + electrolytic refining (zero cyanide, zero mercury process)
- **Integrated Bullion Vault:** Underground treasury vault for 1,000+ MT precious metal storage
- **Trade & Clearing Platform:** Digital and physical settlement exchange
- **Auxiliary Services:** Assay laboratory, security infrastructure, administrative complex, and worker amenities
- **Green Belt:** 33% of total land area (~6.67 hectares) under plantation

**Key Environmental Commitments:**
1. Zero-cyanide, zero-mercury refining process (fire assay + electrolysis)
2. Closed-loop water recycling at 85% efficiency
3. 45 MW power demand met via grid + 15 MW captive solar
4. Comprehensive air pollution control with scrubbers, bag filters, and stacks
5. All hazardous waste (spent carbon, acid sludge, heavy metal precipitate) to CHWTSDF
6. Rainwater harvesting across 100% of built-up area
7. 2.5% of project cost (~₹19.75 crore) allocated to EMP implementation
8. Quarterly post-project environmental monitoring for 5 years

**Category Determination:**
- Schedule 4(a): 330 TPA < 50,000 TPA → prima facie Category B
- Clause 7(c): Capital investment ₹790 crore > ₹100 crore → Category A
- MoEFCC determination requested: Category A due to integrated SEZ status, strategic importance, and investment scale.

### 2.1 Introduction

**2.1.1 Background**
India is the world's 2nd largest consumer of gold, with annual demand exceeding 800 tonnes. Currently, ~95% of gold refining is concentrated in Gujarat (Surat, Ahmedabad) and Tamil Nadu (Coimbatore). Bihar, despite being one of India's fastest-growing economies, has zero large-scale refining capacity. BIBC addresses this gap by establishing a world-class, environmentally responsible bullion complex in Bihar's emerging industrial corridor.

**2.1.2 Need for the Project**
- Regional economic development: Bihar's per-capita income is among India's lowest; BIBC will generate 2,000 direct and 5,000 indirect jobs
- Deconcentration of refining capacity: Reduces single-point vulnerability in Gujarat/Tamil Nadu
- SEZ export potential: Duty-free export of refined gold to Middle East, Southeast Asia, and Africa
- Formalization of gold trade: Brings unorganized refining into regulated, environmentally compliant framework
- Critical minerals strategy: Gold is a strategic reserve metal; domestic refining strengthens supply chain security

**2.2.2 Process Description**

*Step 1: Fire Assay (Sample Analysis)*
- No cyanide used. Lead oxide (litharge) used as flux.
- High-temperature fusion furnace (1,100°C) with bag-filter-equipped stacks.
- Emission: SO2 (negligible), particulate matter (< 50 mg/Nm³ after scrubbing).

*Step 2: Direct Melting & Dore Bar Production*
- Crucibles heated by induction furnaces (electric, not fuel-based).
- No roasting (avoids SO2 generation).
- Emission: PM and NOx (controlled via scrubbers).

*Step 3: Electrolytic Refining (Wohlwill Process)*
- Gold anode dissolved in hydrochloric acid + chlorine gas (or electrolyte).
- Pure gold deposited on cathodes (99.99% purity).
- Acid fumes (HCl, Cl2) captured by fume scrubbers (alkaline scrubbing, 95%+ efficiency).
- Spent electrolyte neutralized with lime and precipitated for heavy metal recovery.

*Step 4: Parting (if required)*
- Nitric acid used for silver separation from dore.
- Acid fumes scrubbed via packed-bed scrubbers.
- Spent acid neutralized; silver recovered as silver nitrate / silver chloride.

### 2.5 Mitigation Measures

**2.5.1 Air Pollution Mitigation**
- All stacks ≥ 30 m height with adequate dispersion
- Bag filters (99% efficiency) on all particulate-generating units
- Wet scrubbers / alkaline scrubbers on acid fume sources
- Fugitive emission control: Enclosed conveyors, vacuum cleaning, water sprinkling
- Green belt: 33% coverage with dust-trapping species (neem, ashoka, gulmohar, eucalyptus)
- Online PM, SO2, NOx, HCl monitoring with real-time data transmission to SPCB and CPCB
- DG sets: Ultra-low sulfur diesel (ULSD) or PNG only; acoustic enclosures; stacks at 6 m height

**2.5.2 Water Pollution Mitigation**
- ETP: Oil & grease trap → equalization → chemical precipitation (for heavy metals) → biological treatment (MBBR) → tertiary filtration (sand + activated carbon) → RO → MEE
- Treated water reuse: Cooling tower makeup, green belt irrigation, dust suppression, toilet flushing
- Domestic sewage: STP (SBR technology) → treated water for gardening
- Rainwater harvesting: 100% rooftop + paved area collection → recharge pits + storage tanks
- Groundwater monitoring: 6 piezometers around site (quarterly sampling for pH, TDS, heavy metals)

**2.5.3 Noise Mitigation**
- Acoustic enclosures on DG sets, compressors, and high-noise equipment
- Vibration isolation pads for furnaces and pumps
- Green belt: 30 m wide noise buffer along boundary (dense plantation)
- Ear protection (PPE) for workers in high-noise zones (> 85 dB(A))
- Periodic audiometric testing for all workers

**2.5.4 Land and Soil Mitigation**
- Spill-proof flooring (acid-resistant tiles) in all chemical handling areas
- Secondary containment (110% volume) for acid tanks and chemical storage
- HDPE-lined effluent collection channels
- Regular soil quality monitoring (quarterly) for heavy metals (As, Cd, Cr, Pb, Hg)
- No open dumping; all waste to designated storage areas

**2.5.5 Biological Mitigation**
- 5,000-tree compensatory plantation (10:1 ratio) in nearby degraded land or BIADA green belt
- Native species: Neem, peepal, banyan, jamun, teak, shisham, amla, bamboo
- 33% green belt within site: Multi-layer plantation (herb, shrub, tree)
- No exotic invasive species; only locally adapted varieties
- Maintenance period: 5 years with 80% survival guarantee

**2.5.6 Socio-Economic Mitigation**
- Land acquisition compensation per LARR Act 2013 (4× market value for rural land)
- Rehabilitation and Resettlement (R&R) package: Housing, employment for one family member, skill training
- Construction phase: Preference to local labor (70% target)
- Community health camp: Quarterly medical camps in surrounding villages
- Education support: School infrastructure upgrade in 5 nearest villages
- Local vendor development: Priority procurement policy for ancillary goods/services

### 4.2 Risk Assessment (QRA)

**Scenario 1: Nitric Acid Tank Rupture**
- Release: 10 MT spill from single tank
- Consequence: Acid mist formation, ground contamination, vegetation damage
- Impact zone: 200 m downwind (IDLH for NO2: 50 ppm)
- Frequency: 1×10⁻⁴ / year (tank failure + containment breach)
- Risk: Moderate (ALARP zone – requires additional mitigation)

**Scenario 2: Chlorine Gas Cylinder Leak**
- Release: 0.5 MT from single cylinder (catastrophic rupture)
- Consequence: Toxic gas cloud
- Impact zone: 500 m downwind (ERPG-2: 3 ppm)
- Frequency: 1×10⁻⁵ / year
- Risk: Low-Moderate (ALARP)

**Scenario 3: Hydrogen Accumulation + Ignition**
- Release: Electrolysis cell hydrogen vent blockage
- Consequence: Flash fire or deflagration
- Impact zone: 10 m (confined space)
- Frequency: 1×10⁻⁶ / year (with proper ventilation)
- Risk: Low (negligible with engineering controls)

**Scenario 4: Fire in HW Storage Area**
- Release: Spontaneous ignition of spent carbon or oily waste
- Consequence: Smoke, toxic fumes, fire spread
- Impact zone: 50 m (site boundary)
- Frequency: 1×10⁻³ / year
- Risk: Moderate (mitigated by fire detection and suppression)

**Scenario 5: Acid Fume Scrubber Failure**
- Release: Uncontrolled HCl/Cl2/NOx emission
- Consequence: Worker exposure, community complaint
- Impact zone: 100 m (plant boundary)
- Frequency: 1×10⁻² / year (with redundancy, reduced to 1×10⁻³)
- Risk: Low (with redundant scrubber)

### 4.3 On-Site Emergency Plan (OEP)

**4.3.1 Emergency Organization**
- **Incident Commander:** Plant Manager
- **Emergency Control Centre (ECC):** Main security building with PA system, backup power, communication
- **Emergency Teams:** Fire fighting, first aid, evacuation, rescue, communication, environment monitoring
- **Shift Emergency Officer:** 24/7 trained shift supervisor

**4.3.2 Emergency Response Procedures**

*Acid Spill:*
1. Alarm activation (manual + automatic leak detection)
2. Containment: Secondary containment holds 110% of largest tank
3. Neutralization: Spill kit with lime/Na2CO3 for acid neutralization
4. Collection: Transfer to neutralization pit
5. Disposal: Treated effluent to ETP; sludge to HW storage
6. Notification: Internal → SPCB → District Emergency Authority (within 1 hour)

*Chlorine Gas Leak:*

**9.2.1 Rooftop Collection**
- GI/HDPE rainwater downpipes from all building roofs
- Collection chambers with filter media (sand + gravel + charcoal) at ground level
- Connected to underground storage tanks or recharge pits

**9.2.2 Storage Tanks**
- Total storage capacity: 2,000 KL (4 tanks × 500 KL each)
- Location: Strategically placed near buildings and green belt
- Material: RCC / HDPE-lined underground tanks
- Use: Green belt irrigation, dust suppression, fire water makeup, toilet flushing

**9.2.3 Recharge Pits / Injection Wells**
- Number: 20 recharge pits (3 m × 3 m × 3 m) along green belt and building periphery
- Design: Brick-lined with filter media (graded sand, gravel, pebbles)
- Recharge rate: 10–15 m³/hr per pit
- Purpose: Replenish groundwater table; maintain aquifer levels

**9.2.4 Stormwater Drains**
- Paved area drains: RCC rectangular drains (0.5 m × 0.5 m) with grated covers
- Oil-water separator at parking area drain outlet
- Silt traps at regular intervals
- All drains connected to recharge pits or storage tanks (no open discharge)

**9.2.5 Percolation Pond**
- One percolation pond (1.0 ha area × 2 m depth) in green belt area
- Natural filtration + recharge during heavy monsoon
- Excess water overflows to secondary recharge zone

### Appendix B: Category A vs. Category B Determination Note

**Submitted for MoEFCC Determination:**

The BIBC project falls under Schedule 4(a) of EIA Notification 2006 – "Metallurgical Industries (Ferrous & Non-Ferrous)". The threshold for Category A is > 50,000 TPA capacity. BIBC's refining capacity is 330 TPA (1,000 kg/day), which is prima facie Category B.

However, we submit that the project should be treated as **Category A** for the following reasons:

1. **Clause 7(c) of EIA Notification:** Capital investment exceeds ₹100 crore (₹790 crore). Projects with investment > ₹100 crore are mandatorily Category A regardless of production capacity.
2. **Integrated SEZ Status:** The project includes a 50-acre SEZ with multi-functional infrastructure (refinery, vault, treasury, trade platform, clearing house). This is not a standalone metallurgical unit but an integrated complex of strategic importance.
3. **Hazardous Chemical Handling:** Despite zero cyanide and zero mercury, the project handles significant quantities of nitric acid, hydrochloric acid, and chlorine gas (total acid storage > 20 MT). The cumulative risk profile warrants EAC scrutiny.
4. **Multi-state Impact:** As a national-level bullion reserve and clearing platform, the project has implications beyond Bihar (financial stability, national security, strategic reserves).
5. **Precedent:** Similar integrated metal complexes (e.g., Vedanta Aluminium, Hindalco) with SEZ status have been treated as Category A even when individual units are below threshold.

**Alternative Position:** If MoEFCC determines Category B, the project will be appraised by the Bihar State Environment Impact Assessment Authority (SEIAA) with ToR issued by the State Expert Appraisal Committee (SEAC). The full EIA report, EMP, DMP, and all annexures remain identical regardless of Category A or B determination.

We request MoEFCC to determine the appropriate category at the earliest to meet the July 20, 2026 deadline.
