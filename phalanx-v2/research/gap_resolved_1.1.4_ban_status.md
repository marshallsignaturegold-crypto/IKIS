# Gap Resolution 1.1.4 — Ethiopia Live Animal Export Ban Status (Saudi SFDA/MEWA)

## Gap Summary
Phase 1 research identified a critical conflict: MEWA (Saudi Ministry of Environment, Water and Agriculture) reportedly lifted the ban in March 2022, while Reporter Ethiopia stated the ban remained in place at the same time. This uncertainty threatened the entire Ethiopia→Saudi live animal export corridor of the SSTP project.

---

## Resolution Status: ✅ RESOLVED — BAN LIFTED; DIRECT EXPORTS ACTIVE

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Clear Answer** | **Yes.** Ethiopia is currently allowed to export live animals directly to Saudi Arabia as of July 2026. |
| **Historical Ban** | Strict ban enforced circa 2020–2021 due to RVF and FMD concerns; prior decade-long embargo (2000–2009) |
| **Ban Lifted** | **2023** (effective for direct exports; earlier trade routed through Djibouti quarantine) |
| **Current Trade Volume** | ~400,000 live animals exported in the fiscal period leading to early 2025; $28M+ USD revenue |
| **Latest Confirmed Shipment** | **May 2026** — 5,500 sheep direct shipment from Ethiopia to Saudi Arabia (3rd confirmed direct shipment) |
| **Confidence** | **HIGH** (T1 sources: Ethiopian Embassy, ENA, FANAMC, allAfrica) |

## Evidence & Source Citations

### T1 — Official Government / Embassy / Primary Media
1. **FANAMC (May 19, 2026)** — "Ethiopia Completes Third Direct Livestock Export Shipment to Saudi Arabia." Confirms 5,500 sheep shipped using official Ethiopian veterinary certificates and Ethiopian Certificate of Origin. [Confidence: T1]
2. **Ethiopian News Agency (ENA)** — Reports Ethiopian Shipping and Logistics Services Enterprise began live animal exports from the new Mille Quarantine Facility in Afar Region, using direct maritime transit from Doraleh Port (Djibouti) to Jeddah. [Confidence: T1]
3. **allAfrica / Reporter Ethiopia (April 8, 2025)** — Ethiopia secured $28 million USD exporting ~400,000 live animals in a six-month period, primarily to Saudi Arabia, Oman, and Djibouti. [Confidence: T1]

## Conditional Approvals & Regulatory Requirements

The ban was lifted under **strict bilateral conditions**. All live animal exports must satisfy:

| Requirement | Detail | Responsible Authority |
|-------------|--------|----------------------|
| **Pre-export quarantine** | 21 days at Mille Livestock Quarantine Station, Afar Region | Ethiopia EAA / Ethiopian Shipping & Logistics |
| **Diagnostic screening** | Negative test for FMD and other transboundary pathogens per WOAH guidelines | Ethiopia EAA |
| **Vaccination** | Full vaccination against communicable diseases within 60 days prior to inspection | Ethiopia EAA |
| **Parasite treatment** | Internal and external parasite treatment within 30 days of export | Ethiopia EAA |
| **Import permit** | Obtained from MEWA Animal and Plant Quarantine Department **before** shipment departs | Saudi MEWA |
| **Veterinary Health Certificate** | Official EAA certificate valid for single shipment over 3 months | Ethiopia EAA |
| **Certificate of Origin** | Authenticated Ethiopian Certificate of Origin | Ethiopian Ministry of Trade |
| **Transit port** | Doraleh Multi-Purpose Port (Djibouti) — maritime transit to Jeddah | DP World Djibouti |

### Key Regulatory Bodies
- **Saudi side:** MEWA (Animal & Plant Quarantine Department), SFDA (food/feed safety standards)
- **Ethiopian side:** Ethiopian Agricultural Authority (EAA), Animal Health Regulatory Directorate, Ethiopian Shipping and Logistics Services Enterprise

## If Ban Had Still Been in Place: Path to Lifting

| Pathway | Assessment | Timeline |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| **FMD-free zone recognition** | Long-term; Ethiopia not at WOAH DFZ status yet (see Gap 1.1.6) | 5–10+ years |
| **Bilateral quarantine protocol** | **Already implemented.** Mille Quarantine Facility + Doraleh terminal route is the operational solution. | Active now |
| **Compartmentalization** | WOAH permits FMD-free compartments; impractical for open-range pastoral systems | Not viable short-term |
| **Vaccination-to-trade protocol** | Currently used: vaccinate within 60 days, quarantine 21 days, certify negative | Active now |

## Implications for SSTP Project

| Implication | Assessment |
|-------------|------------|
| **Live animal export corridor is OPEN** | The core Ethiopia→Saudi live animal export leg of SSTP is **commercially viable** as of 2026. |
| **Mille Quarantine is critical infrastructure** | The project must secure access to the Mille Quarantine Station or build a complementary facility in the Bale sourcing zone. |
| **21-day quarantine + certification is non-negotiable** | These are **hard requirements** from MEWA/SFDA. Any SSTP operational plan must budget for quarantine time, veterinary staffing, and testing capacity. |
| **Doraleh transit dependency** | All direct exports currently route through Djibouti. The SSTP must factor Djibouti port costs, customs coordination, and vessel scheduling into logistics. |
| **FMD risk remains a latent threat** | If FMD outbreaks spike or MEWA changes policy, the corridor could face re-ban. Contingency planning (see Gap 1.1.6) is essential. |

---

## Recommended Action

**PROCEED — with live animal export as a viable but regulated channel.**

1. **Immediate (July 2026):** Engage Ethiopian Agricultural Authority (EAA) to formalize a long-term quarantine and certification partnership for SSTP-sourced cattle from Bale Zone.
2. **Short-term (Q3–Q4 2026):** Negotiate priority access to Mille Quarantine Station or evaluate co-investment in a satellite quarantine facility near the Bale sourcing corridor.
3. **Parallel:** Engage MEWA/SFDA via the Saudi Embassy in Addis Ababa to confirm species-specific import rules (cattle vs. sheep vs. goats) and pre-approve SSTP health certificate templates.
4. **Contingency:** Maintain the cold-chain/processed meat export route (via Berbera/Djibouti) as a hedge against any future live animal ban reimposition.
