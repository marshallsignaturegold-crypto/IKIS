# Stream 1.3.3 — Ethiopia Livestock Supply Chain

**Research Date:** 2025-01-21  
**Confidence Level:** High (based on PMC peer-reviewed research, Ethiopian government data, FAO, USDA-funded studies, and industry reports)

---

## 1. Major Livestock Breeding Zones

Ethiopia's major livestock breeding zones are concentrated in **lowland pastoral and agro-pastoral rangelands**, with highland mixed farming areas also supporting significant cattle populations.

### Key Regions

| Region | Characteristics |
|--------|---------------|
| **Borana (Oromia Regional State)** | Primary source of cattle for export; Boran breed known for fast growth, large body size, and feed conversion efficiency. |
| **Somali Region** | Major source of shoats (sheep and goats) for export; pastoral production systems. |
| **Afar** | Pastoral and agro-pastoral systems; source of cattle and camels. |
| **South Omo** | Livestock production area; part of planned Disease-Free Zones. |
| **Highland Mixed Farming** | High animal densities; predominantly cattle used for local traction and regional trade. |

### Cattle Breeds
- **Boran (Borena) cattle** are the primary indigenous breed for export, known for adaptation to harsh conditions and fast growth.
- Bale lowlands also supply cattle to domestic markets (Bale Robe, Hararghe highlands, Dera, Adama/Nazareth).

## 2. Abattoir Capacity and Certification Status

### Overview
- **Total export-standard abattoirs:** Approximately **14** (per 2021 data from Ethiopian Meat Producers and Exports Association).
- **Aggregate annual capacity:** Approximately **200,000 tons** of sheep, goats, and beef.
- **Capacity utilization:** **Less than 10%** of full capacity due to:
  - Insufficient supply of suitable quality live animals for export market
  - High domestic demand competing for supply
  - Supply chain inefficiencies

### Known Export Abattoirs
1. ELFORA
2. Hashim
3. Luna
4. Mwashi
5. Modjo / Safi
6. Organic Export Abattoir
7. Alnujum Slaughterhouse (Dukem, Oromia)
8. HALAL Foods Industries PLC

### Location
Most export abattoirs are located along the **Addis Ababa–Metahara corridor**, within **45–200 km** from Addis Ababa.

### Certification Status
- **All export abattoirs are halal-certified** or in the process of obtaining halal certification.
- **Most are HACCP-certified** or working toward HACCP certification.
- Some facilities hold **Dubai Municipality approval**.
- Past trade bans have been imposed on some export abattoirs due to poor hygiene and sanitation, causing heavy financial losses.

## 3. Cold Chain Infrastructure

### Current Status
Cold chain infrastructure remains a **major bottleneck**:
- Lack of refrigerated vehicles (reefer trucks)
- Limited freezing and blast-freezer facilities
- Aging packaging systems (most meat export relies on basic stockinette wrapping)
- Inadequate chill facilities at abattoirs
- Temperatures at **Bole Airport** in Addis Ababa are not sufficiently cool (tailored for cut flowers); carcasses often sit on tarmac awaiting loading with no refrigeration
- Only once in air does cargo area achieve adequate cooling temperatures

### Key Initiatives

#### Cool Port Addis (Modjo Dry Port)
- **Investment:** $50 million USD
- **Grant:** 10.6 million euros from the Netherlands
- **Capacity:** Up to 242,000 metric tons of cargo per annum
- **Connection:** Addis-Djibouti railway
- **Primary focus:** Horticultural exports, but capable of integrating meat exports and secondary flows
- **Status:** Under development / launch planned

### Transport Costs
- Transport costs represent roughly **27%** of total marketing costs for live animals.
- Transport costs represent roughly **32%** of total marketing costs for meat exports.
- **Air freight:** USD 700 per ton to Middle East destinations; up to USD 2,500 per ton to other regions.

## 4. Veterinary Infrastructure

### Laboratories
- **National Referral Lab:** Animal Health Institute (AHI)
- **Regional Labs:** 17 regional veterinary laboratories

### Surveillance Systems
- **NADSS** — National Animal Disease Surveillance System
- **ADNIS** — Real-time notification system
- **DOVAR-II** — Monthly reporting system

### Foot-and-Mouth Disease (FMD) Status
- **Highly prevalent, endemic, notifiable disease** that continues to trigger trade bans.
- Government working to establish **Disease-Free Zones (DFZs)** in Borana, South Omo, and Fafan.
- **ET-LITS** (Ethiopia Livestock Identification and Traceability System) being introduced for digital traceability.

### Quarantine Capacity
- **Inadequate** number of quarantine centers to meet **21–30 day** pre-export quarantine requirements for Saudi Arabia and other markets.
- This is a major constraint cited by Ethiopian officials in seeking lifting of Saudi ban.

### SPS Compliance
- Past trade bans imposed due to poor hygiene and sanitation at some export abattoirs.
- SPS compliance remains a **major challenge** for market access.

## 5. Ethiopian Livestock Marketing Enterprise Role

### Institutional Evolution
1. **1998:** Livestock Marketing Agency (LMA) established within Ministry of Trade
2. **Later:** Integrated into Ministry of Agriculture as Livestock and Fishery Marketing Department (LFMD)
3. **2013:** Ethiopian Meat and Dairy Industry Development Institute (EMDIDI)
4. **Current:** **Ethiopian Livestock Development Institute (ELDI)**

### Current Role of ELDI
- Coordinates market access for Ethiopian livestock and meat exports
- Targets illegal cross-border trade
- Promotes **contract-breeding systems** directly linking pastoralists with export abattoirs
- Supports policy reforms and standards development
- Works to increase productivity and competitiveness of livestock value chains

## 6. Export Volumes and Destinations

### Meat Exports (FY ending June 2025)
- **Volume:** 22,620 tonnes of halal-certified meat and by-products
- **Value:** **USD 120 million**
- **Top destinations:**
  - **UAE:** ~51–55% of chilled sheep and goat carcass exports
  - **Saudi Arabia:** ~28–32% of meat exports; also major market for live sheep and goats
  - Other: Egypt, Yemen, Oman, Libya, Djibouti, Sudan

### Live Animal Exports
- **Formal volume (2010/11):** 472,041 head
- **Estimated total value:** USD 190 million (formal channels)
- **Growth rate:** 15–25% per year (historical formal channel growth)
- **Top destinations:** Sudan, Somaliland, Egypt, Djibouti, Saudi Arabia, Yemen, UAE

### Historical Trends

## 7. Cattle Prices, Transport Costs, and Profit Margins

### Cattle and Live Animal Prices

| Animal | 2009 Avg Price (ETB) | 2012 Avg Price (ETB) | Increase |
|--------|---------------------|----------------------|----------|
| Goat | 280 | 860 | 207% |
| Sheep | 340 | 900 | 165% |
| Cattle | 3,400 | 5,400 | 59% |

- **Live weight purchase price at abattoir gate:** ETB 5 per kg (historical benchmark)
- **Export price floor (informal):** USD 500 per head (historically enforced by National Bank concerns)
- **Beef export FOB per ton:** USD 4,197–4,579 (2023–2024 range)
- **Shoat meat FOB per ton:** USD 2,200 (fresh/chilled carcass to Jeddah/Dubai)

### Transport Costs
- **Live animals:** ~27% of total marketing costs
- **Meat exports:** ~32% of total marketing costs
- **Air freight to Middle East:** USD 700 per ton
- **Air freight to other regions:** Up to USD 2,500 per ton
- **Live sheep to Djibouti port:** USD 28.94 per head (total cost at port)

### Profit Margins

#### Shoat (Goat/Sheep) Meat Export
| Metric | Value (USD) |
|--------|-------------|
| FOB Revenue per ton | 2,200.00 |
| Cost per ton | 1,579.76 |
| Profit before tax per ton | 620.24 |
| Profit after tax per ton | 434.17 |
| **Margin before tax** | **28.19%** |
| **Margin after tax** | **19.74%** |

#### Live Sheep Export
| Metric | Value (USD) |
|--------|-------------|
| FOB Djibouti price per head | 30.00 |
| Cost per head at Djibouti port | 28.94 |
| Profit before tax per head | 1.06 |
| Profit after tax per head | 0.74 |
| **Margin before tax** | **3.5%** |
| **Margin after tax** | **2.5%** |

**Note:** Live animal export margins are very low and difficult to sustain without price premiums or volume scale. Ethiopia's comparative advantage lies in **small stock meat rather than live animals**.
