# Gap Resolution 1.3.1 — Berbera Phase 1 Utilization Rate

## Gap Summary
Phase 1 research flagged uncertainty over whether Berbera Port Phase 1 had reached the 75% capacity trigger (375,000 TEU of 500,000 TEU annual capacity). This was critical because Phase 2 expansion (to 2M TEU) is contingent on hitting that trigger. Without Phase 2, the SSTP project's planned cold-chain and containerized meat export corridor through Berbera may face capacity constraints.

## Throughput Trajectory Analysis

### Historical & Current Throughput

| Year | Approx. Annual TEU | % of Phase 1 Capacity | Notes |
|------|-------------------|----------------------|-------|
| Pre-expansion | ~150,000 | 30% | Old port capacity |
| 2024 | ~203,000 | ~40% | +53,000 TEU vs. pre-expansion |
| 2025 (est.) | ~220,000–250,000 | 44–50% | Growth slowed by Red Sea crisis |
| 2026 (proj.) | ~250,000–280,000 | 50–56% | Dependent on regional stability |

### Weekly Traffic Pattern (2025–2026)
- Average weekly container traffic: **1,500–4,900 TEU**
- Annualized range: **78,000–255,000 TEU**
- This wide fluctuation indicates **irregular vessel calls** and **seasonal demand swings**, not a steady growth ramp.

### Growth Constraints
1. **Red Sea shipping crisis (2023–2024–2025)** — Houthi attacks disrupted Suez/Red Sea routing; some shipping lines diverted to Cape of Good Hope, reducing Berbera transshipment demand.
2. **Regional political unrest** — Somaliland recognition disputes and Ethiopia-Somaliland MoU tensions create investor uncertainty.
3. **Ethiopian cargo diversion** — Ethiopia's primary cargo still flows through Djibouti; the Berbera-Ethiopia corridor (road + planned rail) is not yet fully operational.
4. **Lack of Phase 2 trigger** — Without 375K TEU sustained demand, DP World has not committed Phase 2 construction timeline.

## Refrigerated & Cold-Chain Capacity

| Asset | Capacity | Status |
|-------|----------|--------|
| **Reefer plugs (port)** | 336 (expandable to 624) | ✅ Active |
| **BEZ multi-user warehouse** | 10,000 m² | ✅ Opened March 2023 |
| **Dedicated cold-storage warehouses** | Limited / under development | ⚠️ Insufficient for large-scale meat export |
| **Regional cold-chain connectivity** | Weak beyond port/BEZ | ⚠️ Logistical challenge for inland transit |

### Assessment for SSTP Meat Export
- **Containerized frozen/chilled meat:** Phase 1 reefer capacity (336 plugs) is **sufficient for SSTP initial volumes** (estimated 50–100 TEU/month in Year 1).
- **BEZ warehouse:** 10,000 m² can support staging, customs processing, and light processing, but is **not a deep-freeze facility**.
- **Gap:** No large-scale **-25°C cold storage warehouse** at Berbera or BEZ for long-term frozen beef inventory. Djibouti has superior cold-chain infrastructure.

## Implications for SSTP Project

| Implication | Assessment |
|-------------|------------|
| **Phase 1 capacity is NOT a bottleneck** | At 40% utilization, Berbera has ample container throughput headroom for SSTP volumes. |
| **Phase 2 is not needed for SSTP launch** | Project can proceed using Phase 1 infrastructure; no dependency on Phase 2 timeline. |
| **Reefer capacity is adequate initially** | 336 reefer plugs can handle SSTP Year 1–2 volumes; monitor if scaling beyond 200 TEU/month. |
| **Cold-storage gap is real** | Lack of deep-freeze warehouse at Berbera means processed meat should be **containerized immediately** or routed through Djibouti for storage. |
| **Geopolitical risk is elevated** | Red Sea crisis and Somaliland recognition disputes create operational uncertainty. Insurance and contingency routing (Djibouti) are essential. |
| **Ethiopia-Berbera corridor underdeveloped** | Road/rail links from Ethiopia to Berbera are not fully operational; most Ethiopian cargo still uses Djibouti. SSTP must evaluate whether Berbera is the optimal export node vs. Djibouti. |

---

## Recommended Action

**PROCEED — but with Djibouti as primary export node, Berbera as secondary/contingency.**

1. **Immediate (July 2026):** Do **not** make Phase 2 a project dependency. SSTP can launch on Phase 1 capacity.
2. **Route Selection (Q3 2026):**
   - **Primary:** Djibouti (Doraleh Port) — superior cold-chain, established Ethiopian corridor, live animal quarantine terminal.
   - **Secondary:** Berbera — use for diversified routing, price arbitrage, or if Ethiopia-Somaliland corridor matures.
3. **Infrastructure (2026–2027):** If Berbera is retained as a secondary node, evaluate co-investment in a **dedicated cold-storage warehouse** at BEZ or near the port to fill the deep-freeze gap.
4. **Monitor (ongoing):** Track DP World Phase 2 announcements and Red Sea security. If Phase 2 breaks ground (2027+), reassess Berbera's role for scaled exports.
5. **Contingency:** Maintain dual-port contracts (Djibouti + Berbera) to ensure route flexibility if either port faces disruption.
