## 1. DEFINED TERMS

**"AISTA"** means the AISTA Corridor under the Sovereign SafeTrade Program (SSTP).

**"Character Marks"** means trademarks and registrations protecting the names, likenesses, and personas of fictional characters (e.g., "Don Vito," "Vito Corleone") used in branding, media, and merchandise.

**"Copyrights"** means original works of authorship, including content, recipes, training materials, software, and marketing materials, protected under national copyright laws and international treaties (e.g., Berne Convention, TRIPS Agreement).

**"Domain Names"** means internet domain names registered for the protection and promotion of the Marks, including defensive registrations and typosquatting prevention.

**"Enforcement Strategy"** means the systematic approach to monitoring, investigating, and taking action against unauthorized use, infringement, counterfeiting, or dilution of the Intellectual Property.

**"Intellectual Property"** or **"IP"** means all trademarks, copyrights, patents, trade secrets, domain names, social media handles, and other proprietary rights owned or licensed by Corleone Enterprise Holdings and its subsidiaries.

**"IP Valuation"** means the process of estimating the monetary value of the Intellectual Property portfolio, using cost, market, and income approaches.

**"Jurisdiction Strategy"** means the plan for filing, maintaining, and enforcing trademark and IP rights in specific countries and regions, based on market presence, legal framework, and enforcement capability.

**"Licensing Strategy"** means the plan for granting licenses to third parties (franchisees, distributors, media partners, merchandise licensees) to use the Marks and other IP, subject to quality control and brand guidelines.

**"Logo Marks"** means trademarks and registrations protecting the graphical logos, emblems, and design elements used by the Enterprise.

**"Marks"** means the Trademarks, Logo Marks, Character Marks, Product Marks, and Slogan Marks, collectively.

**"Madrid Protocol"** means the Protocol Relating to the Madrid Agreement Concerning the International Registration of Marks (WIPO), allowing for the international registration of trademarks based on a national or regional application or registration.

**"Product Marks"** means trademarks and registrations protecting the names of specific products, product lines, or SKUs (e.g., "Corleone Signature Pizza Sauce," "Corleone Berbera Salt").

**"Slogan Marks"** means trademarks and registrations protecting advertising slogans, taglines, and catchphrases used in branding and marketing.

**"Trade Secrets"** means confidential business information, including recipes, processes, supplier relationships, financial data, and proprietary technology, protected under trade secret laws and contractual obligations.

**"Trademarks"** or **"TM"** means word marks, logo marks, and other distinctive signs used to identify and distinguish the goods and services of the Enterprise from those of others.

**"Word Marks"** means trademarks protecting the textual names and brand names of the Enterprise and its subsidiaries (e.g., "Corleone," "Don Vito's," "Vito Corleone").

## 3. JURISDICTION STRATEGY

### 3.1 Jurisdiction Priority Matrix
The jurisdiction strategy is based on the following factors: (a) current and planned market presence (franchises, CPG distribution, media, concessions); (b) strength of IP legal framework and enforcement capability; (c) availability of Madrid Protocol membership; (d) cost and timeline of registration; and (e) risk of counterfeiting, piracy, and infringement.

| Jurisdiction | Priority | Madrid Member | Filing Route | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **United States (USPTO)** | Tier 1 (Priority) | Yes (origin) | Direct + Madrid origin | Primary market, franchise origin, CPG launch, strong enforcement, Section 15 incontestability |
| **European Union (EUIPO)** | Tier 1 (Priority) | Yes (Madrid designation) | Madrid designation (from US or WIPO) | Large market, single registration for 27 countries, strong border enforcement |
| **United Arab Emirates (UAE TMO)** | Tier 1 (Priority) | Yes (Madrid member since 2023) | Direct + Madrid designation | Key AISTA hub, DMCC operations, franchise and CPG market, strong enforcement, Madrid member |
| **Saudi Arabia (MOCI / SAIP)** | Tier 1 (Priority) | Yes (Madrid member) | Direct + Madrid designation | Key AISTA market, franchise and CPG, halal certification, strong enforcement, Madrid member |
| **India (TM Registry)** | Tier 1 (Priority) | Yes (Madrid member) | Direct + Madrid designation | Large market, Bihar BIBC operations, franchise and CPG, strong enforcement, Madrid member |
| **Kenya (IPO Kenya)** | Tier 2 (High) | No (not Madrid member) | Direct national filing | Franchise and CPG market, regional hub for East Africa, ARIPO member (indirect route) |
| **Qatar (MOCI Qatar)** | Tier 2 (High) | No (not Madrid member) | Direct national filing | Franchise market (Pizza Oven), Gulf hub, strong purchasing power |
| **Egypt (ITDA)** | Tier 2 (High) | Yes (Madrid member) | Madrid designation | Franchise market (Pizza Oven), large population, North Africa hub |
| **Somaliland** | Tier 3 (Medium) | No (not Madrid member) | Direct national filing (if applicable) | SPV operations, concession, mineral extraction, local brand protection |
| **WIPO Madrid Protocol** | Tier 1 (Priority) | N/A | International registration (based on US or EU origin) | Efficient multi-country filing, cost savings, centralized management |
| **Other AISTA / SSTP Markets** | Tier 3 (Medium) | Varies | Madrid designation or direct national filing | Bahrain, Oman, Kuwait, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Ethiopia, Tanzania, Uganda (as market expansion dictates) |

### 3.2 Filing Routes
- **Direct National Filing:** Filing directly with the national trademark office (e.g., USPTO, EUIPO, UAE TMO, India TM Registry). Preferred for high-priority jurisdictions, especially where the mark is first used or where the Enterprise has a significant commercial presence.
- **Madrid Protocol Designation:** Filing an international application through WIPO, designating multiple Madrid member countries based on a national or regional application or registration (the "origin" application). Preferred for cost-efficient multi-country filings, especially for the EU, UAE, Saudi Arabia, India, and Egypt.
- **ARIPO (Africa):** The African Regional Intellectual Property Organization (ARIPO) offers a regional filing system for trademarks in certain African countries (including Kenya). ARIPO may be used as a supplementary route for African markets, but direct national filing is often preferred for Kenya due to local enforcement considerations.

## 5. IP ACQUISITION STRATEGY

### 5.1 Russo Agreement (Fictional Character Clearance)
The Enterprise's brand characters ("Don Vito," "Vito Corleone") are original fictional characters created for the Enterprise. However, the names "Corleone," "Vito Corleone," and "Don Vito" are associated with the characters in Mario Puzo's novel *The Godfather* and Francis Ford Coppola's film series. To minimize the risk of confusion, dilution, or infringement claims, the Enterprise shall:
- (a) Conduct a comprehensive clearance search for all existing trademark registrations, common law uses, and copyright claims related to "Corleone," "Vito Corleone," "Don Vito," "The Godfather," and related marks and characters in all Tier 1 and Tier 2 jurisdictions;
- (b) Engage qualified IP counsel to assess the risk of opposition or infringement claims by Paramount Pictures (owner of the film rights) or the Puzo estate (owner of the literary rights);
- (c) If the risk is material, negotiate a coexistence agreement, consent agreement, or limited license with Paramount and/or the Puzo estate, permitting the Enterprise to use the "Corleone" and "Vito" names in connection with food, beverage, and media products, subject to limitations (e.g., no use of "The Godfather" title, no use of character likenesses from the film, no use of the film's iconic imagery);
- (d) If a coexistence agreement is not feasible, consider rebranding or modifying the marks (e.g., "Corleone Family Kitchen," "Vito's Artisan Bakes") to reduce the risk of confusion; and
- (e) Budget **[$200,000–$500,000]** for clearance, negotiation, and potential settlement or licensing fees.

### 5.2 Third-Party Acquisitions
The Enterprise may acquire trademark portfolios, domain names, or social media handles from third parties to strengthen its brand protection and market presence. Acquisition targets may include:
- (a) Defensive registrations of confusingly similar marks (e.g., "Corleone's," "Don Vito Pizza," "Vito's Bakery") in key jurisdictions;
- (b) Domain names and social media handles that infringe or dilute the Enterprise's marks;
- (c) Product marks or brand names for complementary products or product lines that the Enterprise intends to launch; and
- (d) Local brand names or trademarks in jurisdictions where the Enterprise plans to enter the market.

All acquisitions shall be subject to due diligence, valuation, and Board approval (if the acquisition exceeds [$50,000]).

## 6. ENFORCEMENT STRATEGY

### 6.1 Monitoring
The Enterprise shall implement a comprehensive trademark monitoring program, including:
- (a) **Trademark Watch Services:** Subscribe to trademark watch services (e.g., Thomson Reuters CompuMark, Corsearch, Questel) to monitor new trademark applications, publications, and registrations in all Tier 1, Tier 2, and Tier 3 jurisdictions for marks that are identical or confusingly similar to the Enterprise's marks;
- (b) **Domain Name Monitoring:** Monitor domain name registrations (via WHOIS monitoring and domain watch services) for registrations that incorporate the Enterprise's marks or confusingly similar terms;
- (c) **Social Media Monitoring:** Monitor social media platforms (Instagram, Facebook, Twitter/X, TikTok, YouTube) for unauthorized use of the Enterprise's marks, logos, or character designs;
- (d) **Marketplace Monitoring:** Monitor e-commerce platforms (Amazon, Noon, Souq, Flipkart, Jumia) and online marketplaces for counterfeit or infringing products bearing the Enterprise's marks; and
- (e) **Internet and Search Engine Monitoring:** Use automated tools and manual searches to monitor internet use, websites, and search engine results for unauthorized use of the Enterprise's marks.

### 6.2 Cease and Desist
If unauthorized use is detected, the Enterprise shall take the following escalating enforcement actions:
- (a) **Initial Contact:** Send a cease and desist letter (C&D) from the Enterprise's legal counsel or local trademark agent, demanding the infringer cease use, transfer any infringing domain names or social media handles, and provide an accounting of profits;
- (b) **Negotiation:** If the infringer responds, negotiate a resolution (e.g., coexistence agreement, consent agreement, assignment of the infringing mark, or rebrand);
- (c) **Administrative Action:** If the infringer has filed a conflicting trademark application, file an opposition or cancellation proceeding before the relevant trademark office (e.g., TTAB in the US, EUIPO Opposition Division, local trademark office in other jurisdictions);
- (d) **Domain Name Dispute:** If the infringement involves a domain name, file a UDRP (Uniform Domain-Name Dispute-Resolution Policy) or URS (Uniform Rapid Suspension) proceeding before WIPO or another approved dispute resolution provider;
- (e) **Social Media Takedown:** If the infringement involves social media content, file a takedown request under the platform's terms of service (e.g., Instagram/Facebook IP infringement report, Twitter/X DMCA takedown, TikTok IP infringement report);
- (f) **Marketplace Takedown:** If the infringement involves counterfeit products on e-commerce platforms, file a takedown request under the platform's brand protection program (e.g., Amazon Brand Registry, Noon Brand Protection, Flipkart Brand Registry); and
- (g) **Customs Recordation:** Record the Enterprise's trademark registrations with customs authorities in key jurisdictions (e.g., US Customs and Border Protection, EU Customs, UAE Customs, Saudi Customs, India Customs) to enable the seizure of counterfeit or infringing goods at the border.

### 6.3 Litigation
If administrative and voluntary enforcement actions are unsuccessful, the Enterprise may pursue litigation in the relevant jurisdiction, including:
- (a) **Trademark Infringement Actions:** File a lawsuit in the national courts (e.g., US federal court, EU national courts, UAE courts, Saudi commercial courts, Indian High Courts) for trademark infringement, dilution, and unfair competition;
- (b) **Border Enforcement Actions:** Coordinate with customs authorities to seize counterfeit goods and pursue criminal or civil penalties against counterfeiters;
- (c) **Criminal Actions:** In jurisdictions where trademark counterfeiting is a criminal offense (e.g., UAE, Saudi Arabia, India), work with law enforcement and prosecutors to pursue criminal charges against counterfeiters; and
- (d) **Arbitration:** If the dispute arises from a franchise agreement, distribution agreement, or licensing agreement, pursue arbitration under the applicable dispute resolution clause.

Litigation shall be subject to Board approval (if the estimated cost exceeds [$100,000]) and shall be managed by qualified IP litigation counsel in each jurisdiction.

### 6.4 Customs Recordation
The Enterprise shall record its trademark registrations with customs authorities in all jurisdictions where the Enterprise has a significant commercial presence or where counterfeit goods are likely to enter the market. Customs recordation enables customs officers to detain and seize counterfeit goods at the border, providing a powerful enforcement tool. The Enterprise shall:
- (a) File customs recordation applications in the US (CBP), EU (EU Customs), UAE (Dubai Customs, Abu Dhabi Customs), Saudi Arabia (Saudi Customs), India (Central Board of Indirect Taxes and Customs), Kenya (Kenya Revenue Authority), and other jurisdictions;
- (b) Provide customs authorities with product identification guides, images, and authentication features to assist in identifying counterfeit goods;
- (c) Coordinate with customs authorities to conduct training and awareness programs on the Enterprise's brands and products; and
- (d) Monitor customs seizures and take action to dispose of seized goods (destruction, donation, or recycling).

## 7. LICENSING STRATEGY

### 7.1 Franchise Trademark License
The Enterprise shall license the Don Vito's Pizza Oven and Vito Corleone Bakery & Cafe marks to franchisees under the Franchise Agreement Templates (`franchise_agreement_template_pizza.md`, `franchise_agreement_template_bakery.md`). The license shall be:
- Limited to the Territory and Term of the franchise agreement;
- Non-exclusive (the Franchisor retains the right to use the marks and license other franchisees);
- Non-transferable (except with the Franchisor's consent);
- Subject to quality control and brand guidelines; and
- Revocable upon termination or breach of the franchise agreement.

### 7.2 CPG Licensing
The Enterprise shall license the Corleone Products marks and product marks to distributors, co-manufacturers, and retailers under the CPG Distribution Agreement Template (`cpg_distribution_agreement_template.md`). The license shall be:
- Limited to the Territory and channels specified in the distribution agreement;
- Non-exclusive (unless otherwise agreed for a specific channel or territory);
- Subject to quality control, product specifications, and packaging guidelines; and
- Revocable upon termination or breach of the distribution agreement.

### 7.3 Media Content Licensing
Vito Corleone Media & Licensing LLC shall license media content (e.g., brand storytelling videos, character animations, recipes, training materials) to franchisees, distributors, and third-party media platforms. The license shall be:
- Limited to the specific content, territory, and use (e.g., in-store display, social media, online advertising);
- Subject to quality control and content guidelines;
- Royalties or flat fees, as negotiated; and
- Revocable upon termination or breach.

### 7.4 Merchandise Licensing
The Enterprise may license the Marks, Logo Marks, and Character Marks for merchandise (e.g., apparel, kitchenware, accessories, toys, collectibles) to third-party merchandise licensees. Merchandise licensing agreements shall:
- Require the licensee to maintain quality control and brand standards;
- Limit the Territory and product categories;
- Require the licensee to submit product samples and packaging for approval;
- Include minimum sales guarantees and royalty payments (typically **[8–15%]** of net sales); and
- Include audit rights and termination provisions for breach or underperformance.

### 7.5 Licensing Governance
All licensing agreements shall be reviewed and approved by the General Counsel and the Board (if the agreement exceeds [$100,000] in annual royalties or covers a new territory or product category). The Enterprise shall maintain a licensing register, tracking all licensees, territories, product categories, royalty rates, and termination dates.

## 9. TRADE SECRET STRATEGY

### 9.1 Trade Secret Identification
The Enterprise shall identify and protect the following categories of trade secrets and confidential business information:
- (a) **Recipes and Formulations:** Proprietary recipes, ingredient ratios, flavor profiles, and preparation methods for all menu items, CPG products, and coffee blends;
- (b) **Processes and Methods:** Proprietary manufacturing processes, halal processing techniques, cold chain management protocols, and quality control procedures;
- (c) **Supplier Relationships:** Supplier lists, pricing, terms, and exclusive supplier relationships;
- (d) **Customer Data:** Customer lists, loyalty program data, purchasing habits, and demographic information;
- (e) **Financial Data:** Financial projections, budgets, pricing strategies, margin data, and cost structures;
- (f) **Technology:** Proprietary software source code, algorithms, and system architecture;
- (g) **Business Strategies:** Market expansion plans, competitive intelligence, and strategic partnerships; and
- (h) **Government Relationships:** Concession terms, government contacts, and negotiation strategies.

### 9.2 Trade Secret Protection Measures
The Enterprise shall implement the following measures to protect trade secrets:
- (a) **Confidentiality Agreements:** All employees, contractors, franchisees, distributors, suppliers, and JV partners shall sign confidentiality and non-disclosure agreements (NDAs) that clearly define trade secrets, prohibit unauthorized use and disclosure, and specify the consequences of breach;
- (b) **Access Controls:** Trade secrets shall be stored in secure, access-controlled locations (physical and digital). Access shall be limited to employees and contractors with a legitimate need to know. Digital trade secrets shall be encrypted, password-protected, and stored on secure servers with access logs;
- (c) **Marking and Labeling:** Trade secret documents shall be marked as "CONFIDENTIAL — TRADE SECRET" and shall be subject to document control and version management (`document_control_version_management.md`);
- (d) **Exit Procedures:** Upon termination of employment or contract, all individuals with access to trade secrets shall return all confidential materials and confirm in writing that they have destroyed all copies and retained no trade secret information;
- (e) **Training:** All employees and contractors with access to trade secrets shall receive annual training on trade secret protection, including the identification of trade secrets, the importance of confidentiality, and the consequences of misappropriation;
- (f) **Physical Security:** Facilities where trade secrets are stored or used (e.g., test kitchens, R&D labs, manufacturing plants) shall be secured with access controls, surveillance, and visitor logs; and
- (g) **IT Security:** Digital trade secrets shall be protected by firewalls, intrusion detection systems, anti-malware software, and regular security audits. Remote access shall be restricted to VPN connections with multi-factor authentication.

### 9.3 Trade Secret Enforcement
In the event of trade secret misappropriation (theft, unauthorized disclosure, or use by a competitor or former employee), the Enterprise shall:
- (a) Conduct a forensic investigation to identify the source and extent of the misappropriation;
- (b) Seek injunctive relief in the relevant courts to prevent further disclosure or use;
- (c) Pursue damages (actual damages, unjust enrichment, or reasonable royalty) under the applicable trade secret law (e.g., US Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA), EU Trade Secrets Directive, local trade secret laws in UAE, Saudi Arabia, India, Kenya);
- (d) Report criminal trade secret theft to law enforcement authorities (if applicable); and
- (e) Review and strengthen trade secret protection measures to prevent recurrence.

## 11. IP VALUATION

### 11.1 Valuation Approaches
The Enterprise shall conduct periodic valuations of its Intellectual Property portfolio to support financial reporting, investor relations, M&A transactions, and licensing negotiations. The valuation shall be conducted by a qualified IP valuation firm or financial advisor using one or more of the following approaches:

### 11.2 Cost Approach
The cost approach estimates the value of IP based on the cost to recreate or replace the IP. This approach is most appropriate for trademarks and copyrights that are not yet generating significant revenue, or for defensive marks that are not actively licensed. The cost approach includes:
- **Historical Cost:** The actual costs incurred to develop, register, and maintain the IP (filing fees, legal fees, design fees, development costs);
- **Replacement Cost:** The estimated cost to recreate the IP from scratch (e.g., the cost to develop a new brand, design a new logo, or create a new character); and
- **Reproduction Cost:** The estimated cost to reproduce the IP in its current form (e.g., the cost to re-register all trademark applications, re-create all copyrightable works).

### 11.3 Market Approach
The market approach estimates the value of IP based on comparable market transactions (e.g., sales of similar trademarks, licensing rates for similar brands, M&A transactions involving similar IP). The market approach requires access to comparable transaction data, which may be limited for unique brands and characters. The market approach includes:
- **Comparable Transactions:** Analysis of trademark sales, brand acquisitions, and licensing deals in the food and beverage industry;
- **Royalty Rates:** Analysis of industry-standard royalty rates for franchise, CPG, and media licensing; and
- **Brand Value Rankings:** Reference to brand value rankings (e.g., Interbrand, Brand Finance) for comparable brands in the food and beverage sector.

### 11.4 Income Approach
The income approach estimates the value of IP based on the present value of future economic benefits generated by the IP (e.g., royalties, licensing fees, incremental profits, cost savings). The income approach is most appropriate for revenue-generating IP (e.g., franchise trademarks, CPG product marks, media content). The income approach includes:
- **Relief from Royalty:** Estimates the value of the IP by calculating the hypothetical royalty payments that the Enterprise would save by owning the IP rather than licensing it from a third party. The royalty rate is based on industry benchmarks and comparable licensing agreements.
- **Incremental Profits:** Estimates the value of the IP by calculating the incremental profits attributable to the IP (e.g., the additional revenue and profit generated by the Don Vito's brand compared to a generic pizza brand).
- **Excess Earnings:** Estimates the value of the IP by calculating the excess earnings generated by the IP above a normal return on tangible assets.

### 11.5 Contribution to Enterprise Value
The IP valuation shall be integrated into the Enterprise's overall enterprise valuation. The IP portfolio is expected to contribute significantly to the Enterprise's enterprise value, particularly as the brand recognition and franchise network expand. The IP valuation shall be updated:
- Annually for financial reporting and investor relations;
- Prior to each financing round (Seed, Series A, B, C) and the IPO;
- In connection with M&A transactions, joint ventures, or licensing negotiations; and
- In connection with litigation or dispute resolution involving the IP.

### 11.6 IP Valuation Budget
A budget of **[$100,000–$200,000]** shall be allocated for the initial IP valuation (conducted by a qualified valuation firm) and for periodic updates. The valuation shall be reviewed and approved by the Board and the Audit Committee.
